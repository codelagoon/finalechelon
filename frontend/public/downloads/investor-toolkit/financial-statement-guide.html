Echelon Equity Financial Statement Guide

A Beginner's Guide to Reading Company Financials

Income Statement

The income statement shows a company's financial performance over a period of time (quarterly or annually). It tells you how much money the company made or lost.

Key Sections:

  • Revenue: Total money earned from selling goods or services
  • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): Direct costs to produce the goods/services sold
  • Gross Profit: Revenue minus COGS
  • Operating Expenses: SG&A, R&D, marketing, administrative costs
  • Operating Income (EBIT): Gross profit minus operating expenses
  • Interest & Taxes: Interest expense and income taxes
  • Net Income: The bottom line profit after all expenses

What to look for: Revenue growth trends, margin stability, and consistent profitability.

Balance Sheet

The balance sheet shows what a company owns (assets) and what it owes (liabilities) at a specific point in time. It must always balance: Assets = Liabilities + Equity.

Key Sections:

  • Current Assets: Cash, accounts receivable, inventory (assets convertible to cash within 1 year)
  • Non-Current Assets: Property, plant, equipment, intangible assets
  • Current Liabilities: Accounts payable, short-term debt (due within 1 year)
  • Long-Term Liabilities: Long-term debt, deferred tax liabilities
  • Shareholders' Equity: Common stock, retained earnings

What to look for: Debt levels, cash position, and working capital management.

Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement shows how cash moves in and out of the company during a period. It's divided into three sections.

Key Sections:

  • Operating Cash Flow: Cash generated from core business operations
  • Investing Cash Flow: Cash spent on or received from investments (CapEx, acquisitions)
  • Financing Cash Flow: Cash from or paid to investors (debt, equity, dividends)

What to look for: Positive operating cash flow, sustainable capital spending, and financing strategy.

Management Discussion & Analysis (MD&A)

The MD&A is management's narrative explanation of the financial results. It provides context that numbers alone cannot convey.

What to Read:

  • Management's explanation of revenue changes
  • Discussion of major risks and uncertainties
  • Outlook and future guidance
  • Significant events or transactions

Common Financial Ratios

Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio
P/E = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share
Measures how much investors are willing to pay for each dollar of earnings. Lower P/E may indicate undervaluation or higher risk.
Debt-to-Equity Ratio
D/E = Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity
Indicates financial leverage. Higher ratios mean more debt relative to equity, which can increase risk but also potential returns.
Operating Margin
Operating Margin = Operating Income / Revenue
Shows profitability from core operations before interest and taxes. Higher margins indicate better operational efficiency.
Working Capital
Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities
Measures short-term liquidity. Positive working capital means the company can cover short-term obligations. Negative may indicate liquidity stress.

Tips for Beginners

  • Always read multiple years of data to identify trends
  • Compare ratios to industry peers and historical averages
  • Look beyond the numbers—read the footnotes and MD&A
  • Be skeptical of one-time items that may distort results
  • Remember that past performance doesn't guarantee future results
